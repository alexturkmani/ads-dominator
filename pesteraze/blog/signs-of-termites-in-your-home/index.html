Why Early Termite Detection Matters

Termites are often called "silent destroyers" for good reason. These tiny insects can cause significant structural damage to your home before you even know they're there. In Australia, termites cause more damage to homes than fires, floods, and storms combined, with repair costs often reaching tens of thousands of dollars.

The key to protecting your property is early detection. By learning to recognize the warning signs of termite activity, you can catch an infestation before it becomes a major problem. In this comprehensive guide, our termite inspection experts share the 10 most common signs of termites in Australian homes.

1. Mud Tubes on Walls and Foundations

Mud tubes (also called shelter tubes) are one of the most telltale signs of termite activity. Subterranean termites, the most common species in Sydney, build these pencil-sized tubes to travel between their nest and food sources while staying protected from predators and maintaining humidity.

Where to look: Check your foundation walls, crawl spaces, external walls, and anywhere wood meets the ground. Mud tubes may also appear on internal walls, particularly in bathrooms and kitchens where moisture is present.

What they look like: Brown, dried mud tubes about 10-25mm wide, running vertically up walls or along horizontal surfaces.

2. Hollow-Sounding Wood

Termites eat wood from the inside out, leaving a thin veneer of timber or paint. When you tap on termite-damaged wood, it will often sound hollow or papery because the inside has been consumed.

How to check: Use the handle of a screwdriver to tap along wooden surfaces including skirting boards, door frames, window frames, and structural timbers. Compare the sound to areas you know are solid.

Related issues: You might also notice that wood crumbles easily when probed, or that paint has tiny holes where termites have eaten through to the surface.

3. Discarded Termite Wings

Once a year (usually in spring or after rain), mature termites develop wings and swarm to start new colonies. After landing and finding a mate, they shed their wings. Finding piles of discarded wings is a sign that termites are actively establishing new colonies nearby.

Where to look: Window sills, doorways, light fittings, spider webs, and other areas where flying insects might be attracted to light.

What they look like: Tiny, translucent wings about 6-10mm long, all roughly the same size. Unlike flying ants, termite wings are equal in length.

Learn more about the differences in our guide: Termites vs Flying Ants: How to Tell the Difference

4. Frass (Termite Droppings)

Drywood termites (less common in Sydney but still present) create small pellets of excrement called frass. As they eat through wood, they push these pellets out through small holes, creating tiny piles that look like sawdust or sand.

What it looks like: Small, wood-colored pellets about 1mm long, often in small mounds near wooden structures.

Note: Subterranean termites don't produce visible frass as they use their droppings to build mud tubes. If you see frass, you likely have drywood termites.

5. Bubbling or Peeling Paint

When termites feed close to the surface of wood or drywall, they can cause moisture to build up between the paint and the surface. This leads to bubbling, blistering, or peeling paint that looks similar to water damage.

Where to check: Painted wooden surfaces, particularly skirting boards, door frames, and walls.

What to do: If you notice unexplained paint damage, especially in combination with other signs, contact a professional for an inspection.

6. Tight-Fitting Doors and Windows

As termites consume wood in door and window frames, they produce moisture through their activity. This moisture can cause the wood to warp, making doors and windows suddenly difficult to open or close.

Warning: While tight doors and windows can have other causes (humidity, settling), if you notice this in combination with other termite signs, it warrants investigation.

7. Visible Termite Swarmers

Seeing actual termites is an obvious sign of infestation. Termite swarmers (alates) are winged termites that emerge to start new colonies. They're often confused with flying ants but have distinct differences.

Termite identification:

  • Straight, beaded antennae (ants have elbowed antennae)
  • Thick waist (ants have pinched waists)
  • Four wings of equal size (ants have larger front wings)

For detailed identification, see our pest identification guide.

8. Sagging Floors or Ceilings

In severe infestations, termites can weaken structural timbers enough to cause visible sagging in floors, ceilings, or roof lines. This is a serious sign that significant damage has already occurred.

Action required: If you notice sagging structures, seek immediate professional inspection. This level of damage may require structural repairs in addition to termite treatment.

9. Clicking Sounds in Walls

Soldier termites bang their heads against wood or shake their bodies when the colony is disturbed, creating a clicking or rattling sound. Worker termites also make noise while eating, which some describe as "crunching."

How to listen: Press your ear against walls, particularly at night when the house is quiet. You may hear faint clicking or rustling sounds.

10. Tunnels or Galleries in Wood

If you have access to exposed wood (such as in a crawl space, basement, or during renovations), look for the tunnels that termites create as they eat through timber. These galleries run along the grain of the wood.

What they look like: Clean, smooth tunnels running parallel to the wood grain, sometimes with dried mud lining.

What to Do If You Spot These Signs

Step 1: Don't Disturb the Termites

If you find termite activity, resist the urge to spray insecticide or disturb the area. This can cause the termites to scatter and establish new colonies elsewhere, making treatment more difficult. Leave everything as you found it.

Step 2: Document What You've Found

Take photos and note the location of any signs you've discovered. This will help the pest control professional during their inspection.

Step 3: Contact a Licensed Termite Professional

Termite treatment requires specialized knowledge and equipment. A licensed technician will conduct a thorough inspection and recommend appropriate treatment options. At Pest Eraze, we offer:

  • Comprehensive termite inspections with thermal imaging
  • Multiple treatment options including barriers and baiting
  • Detailed reports for your records
  • Ongoing protection programs

Step 4: Consider Prevention

Even if the inspection finds no active termites, consider implementing preventive measures. Regular inspections and termite barriers can save you thousands in potential repair costs.

Termite Prevention Tips

While professional protection is essential, there are steps you can take to reduce termite risk:

  1. Reduce moisture: Fix leaky taps, pipes, and air conditioning units. Ensure proper drainage away from your foundation.
  2. Remove wood-to-soil contact: Keep timber, mulch, and debris away from your home's foundation.
  3. Improve ventilation: Ensure subfloor areas have adequate ventilation to reduce moisture.
  4. Store firewood away: Keep woodpiles at least 20 meters from your home.
  5. Regular inspections: Schedule annual professional inspections.

For more prevention advice, see our guide: Complete Guide to Termite Prevention

Conclusion

Termite infestations are a serious threat to Australian homes, but early detection can prevent major damage and costly repairs. By learning to recognize these 10 warning signs and scheduling regular professional inspections, you can protect your property and enjoy peace of mind.
